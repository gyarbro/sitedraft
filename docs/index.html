Mrs. Yarbrough's Background

I hold an English undergraduate degree from Old Dominion University with a concentration in secondary education (grades 6-12). I am also in the process of obtaining my Curriculum and Instruction graduate degree from George Mason University with an emphasis in Dual Enrollment English higher education.

I have been teaching English since 2020. I taught English 9 and 11 Academic at Kempsville High School from Virginia Beach City Public Schools, and I have also taught 8th grade English/Language Arts at Smart’s Mill Middle School from Loudoun County Public Schools.

I have been an English teacher at Freedom High School since 2022. Throughout my time at Freedom, I have been fortunate enough to teach English 9 Honors, English 10 Academic and Honors, English 12 Academic, Creative Writing, and Public Speaking.

In addition to my teaching experience, I have sponsored the Hispanic/Latino Affinity group, Holiday Club, Debate and Forensics team, Students Against Violence Everywhere (SAVE), and the Competitive Programming Team (CPT). I am also one of the current co-sponsors of the Freedom High School (FHS) chapter of the National Honor Society (NHS), and a Sources of Strength (SOS)/Unified Mental Health Team (UMHT) Advisor.

For fun, my family enjoys spending time with one another. We love being near the water; needless to say, we miss living in Virginia Beach. However, my husband (Mr. Yabrough) and I grew up in Manassas, VA; therefore, Northern Virginia (or NoVA) is nothing new to us. Yes, Mr. Yarbrough is a Biology and Chemistry teacher at FHS. We are fortunate enough to work alongside each other. We are excited to see what the future holds for us.

Mrs. Yarbrough's Teaching Philosophy

My teaching philosophy centers on student empowerment, respect, and adaptability. I believe that every student deserves access to a high-quality education and that all learners are capable of growth. Mistakes are not setbacks, but essential steps in building resilience and confidence. In our classroom, I embrace flexibility, adjusting instruction to meet the unique needs, strengths, and dynamics of each group of students. Every class is a new journey, and I see my role as a guide who supports, challenges, and adapts alongside my learners.

As a parent, I strongly value respect and accountability. These principles shape how I engage with my students. I work to earn students' respect by modeling integrity, compassion, and fairness. I also understand that life happens, and I support students through challenges while holding them to consistent expectations. Academically, I set high standards for all learners and use assessment data and observation to tailor instruction and support. I aim to create a structured yet nurturing environment where students feel safe to take academic risks, explore their interests, and develop a sense of ownership over their learning.

Student voice and choice are key to my classroom. I provide opportunities for students to shape their learning experiences and express their individuality through projects, discussions, and reflective practices. This not only builds engagement but fosters lifelong learning habits.

I also believe that education works best when it’s a team effort. I view families as essential partners and maintain open, ongoing communication to support each child’s growth. Lastly, I remain committed to refining my own practice. I seek out professional development (such as being involved with the summer English curriculum team), collaborating with colleagues, and learning from my students every day.

Course Overview

In tenth grade, students strengthen their reading and thinking skills by exploring different kinds of fiction and nonfiction texts. They learn to understand important themes and use information from multiple sources to solve problems and answer questions. Vocabulary and language skills continue to grow.

Students focus on writing that persuades and analyzes, using evidence to support their ideas. They also learn about media by creating and evaluating messages they see every day. Research skills are built by finding trustworthy information and properly giving credit to sources. Students work both on their own and with others to improve their communication and presentation skills.

All lessons follow the Virginia Department of Education’s Standards of Learning (SOLs) to make sure students meet important academic goals.

Course Units:

Our course units guide students through four engaging and thematically rich texts that explore human nature, ambition, injustice, and the power of language. Each quarter features a whole-class novel aligned with a StudySync unit, providing historical context, literary depth, and opportunities for critical thinking. Together, these selections foster deep reading, discussion, and analysis across fiction, drama, and nonfiction.

In addition to the whole-class texts, students are responsible for reading at least one independent book of their choice each quarter. A library visit will be provided at the beginning of each quarter to help students select their books. At times, students may be asked to make connections between their independent reading and the themes of the unit’s core text.

An overview of our course units is provided below:

Quarter One: Frankenstein by Mary Shelley
StudySync Unit: Human Nature
Whole Class Novel: Frankenstein by Mary Shelley
Goodreads Rating: 3.89/5

Summary: Frankenstein is Mary Shelley's groundbreaking novel about a scientist whose attempt to create life results in a tragic and terrifying creation. This edition presents the original 1818 text, highlighting the novel’s powerful themes, political edge, and emotional depth. It also includes expert commentary, historical context, and further reading to deepen understanding of this timeless classic.

Quarter Two: Macbeth by William Shakespeare
StudySync Unit: Origin Stories
Whole Class Novel: Macbeth by William Shakespeare
Goodreads Rating: 4.31/5

Summary: Macbeth is a dramatic tale of ambition and betrayal, where a Scottish nobleman is driven by prophecy to murder his way to the throne. As he gains power, guilt, ghosts, and rebellion begin to unravel his world. This gripping story explores how the thirst for power can lead to destruction and downfall.

Quarter Three: Killers of the Flower Moon by David Grann
StudySync Unit: Chopped, Stirred, and Blended
Whole Class Novel: Killers of the Flower Moon by David Grann
Goodreads Rating: 4.14/5

Summary: Killers of the Flower Moon tells the true story of the Osage Nation in 1920s Oklahoma, who became incredibly wealthy after oil was discovered on their land. Their prosperity turned tragic when members of the Osage community, including Mollie Burkhart’s family, were systematically murdered in a chilling plot to steal their wealth. The case drew national attention and led to one of the FBI’s first major investigations, uncovering a shocking conspiracy that changed the course of American justice.

Quarter Four: 1984 by George Orwell
StudySync Unit: The Power of Communication
Whole Class Novel: 1984 by George Orwell
Goodreads Rating: 4.20/5

Summary: 1984 by George Orwell is a powerful novel about a dystopian society where the government controls every aspect of life, including truth, memory, and individual thought. The story follows a man who risks everything for love and freedom in a world where surveillance is constant and rebellion is dangerous. This classic book introduces terms like "Big Brother" and "Thought Police" and remains a relevant warning about the loss of freedom and personal identity.
